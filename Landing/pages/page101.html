National River Linking Project (NRLP):

What is Inter-linking of Rivers (ILR)?

The interlinking of river project is a Civil Engineering project, which aims to connect Indian rivers through reservoirs and canals.
The project envisages the interlinking of 37 rivers in India by a network of almost 3000 storage dams. This will form a gigantic South Asian water grid.

Brief History:

The Inter-linking of Rivers in India proposal has a long history. During British colonial rule, for example, the 19th-century engineer Arthur Cotton proposed the plan to interlink major Indian rivers to hasten the import and export of goods from its colony in South Asia, as well as to address water shortages and droughts in south-eastern India, now Andhra Pradesh and Orissa.
After Independence, successive governments either mooted the idea of interlinking the rivers or some governments turned down the idea. During the beginning of the 21st century, the inter-linking of rivers came to the forefront, but again, was not implemented because of the project of the Golden Quadrilateral, a network of highways connecting India's four top metropolitan cities, namely Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai and Kolkata, became more important than interlinking of rivers. The present Government of India is seriously thinking of interlinking rivers.

There are two components to this project:

1. Himalayan Component
2. Peninsular Component

The Indo-Gangetic rivers are perennial since they are fed by rain as well as the glaciers from the Himalayas.
The peninsular rivers in India are, however, not seasonal because they are rain-fed mainly by the southwest Monsoons.
Due to this, the Indo-Gangetic plains suffer from floods and the peninsular states suffer from droughts.
Hence, the idea of interlinking rivers is to bring about an equitable distribution of river waters in India.

Benefits of River Interlinking:

  1. Interlinking rivers is a way to transfer excess water from the regions which receive a lot of rainfall to drought-prone areas. This way, it can control both floods and droughts
  2. The project will also help with hydropower generation as many plants can be set up.
  3. Indian agriculture is primarily monsoon-dependent. This leads to problems with agricultural output when the monsoons behave unexpectedly. The project will provide irrigation facilities in water-deficient places.
  4. The project will also help commercially because of the betterment of the inland waterways transport system.
  5. Moreover, the rural areas will have an alternate source of income in the form of fish farming, etc.
  6. The project will also augment the defense and security of the country through additional waterline defense.

Challenges in River Interlinking:


Project feasibility: The project is estimated to cost around Rs.5.6 lakh crores. Additionally, there is also the requirement of huge structures. All this requires a great engineering capacity. So, the cost and manpower requirements are immense.

Environmental impact: river flow naturally, interlinking and, diverting the rivers' flow causes interference with nature and hence it may threaten the natural ecosystem. The huge project will alter entire ecosystems. The wildlife, flora, and fauna of the river systems will suffer because of such displacements and modifications. Many national parks and sanctuaries fall within the river systems. All these considerations will have to be taken care of while implementing the project. The project could reduce the flow of fresh water into the sea, thus, affecting marine aquatic life. Interlinking demands the construction of many dams which take up a lot of land and parts of the forest in some cases.

Impact on society: Building dams and reservoirs will cause the displacement of a lot of people. This will cause a lot of agony for a lot of people. They will have to be rehabilitated and adequately compensated.

Controlling floods: Some people express doubts as to the capability of this project to control floods. Although theoretically, it is possible, Indiaâ€™s experience has been different. There have been instances where big dams like Hirakud Dam, Damodar Dam, etc. have brought flooding to Odisha, West Bengal, etc.

Inter-state disputes: Many states like Kerala, Sikkim, Andhra Pradesh, etc. have opposed river interlinking.

International disputes: In the Himalayan component of the project, the effect of building dams and interlinking rivers will affect the neighbouring countries. This will have to be factored in while implementing the project. Bangladesh has opposed the transfer of water from the Brahmaputra to the Ganga.

Conclusion:

Though interlinking rivers is an excellent idea with flaws as it requires a lot of issues to be addressed before being implemented.
